Introduction

For a moment, step into the shoes of Brisha Borden, a Floridian on a mission to retrieve her god-sister. Unfortunately, you’re already late; so it’s not a hard choice for you to take advantage of an unsupervised bike in an effort to recover lost time.The vehicle proves to be too small and you leave it behind, despite the situation’s urgency. After abandoning the bike, you are promptly arrested for burglary and petty theft of items valued around $80.


Around the same time in 2014, seasoned criminal Vernon Prater is arrested for petty theft of $86.35 worth of items at a nearby Home Depot. Two years later, Prater is serving 8 years in prison for stealing thousands of dollars worth of electronics, and you have yet to reoffend- so why did an AI-powered risk assessment label you as 166% more likely to commit future crime than Prater? After comparing Prater’s history of armed robberies to your history of juvenile misdemeanors, shouldn’t he be considered more dangerous?




What is AI?

Get comfy in your own shoes again, and think about that scenario. Artificial intelligence, often referred to as AI, is a catch-all term for digital systems designed to convincingly match human intelligence. Using a method called machine learning, modern AI models generate a response based on an algorithm (step-by-step process) shaped by past data. AI models are not infallible, but developers hone them by feeding their models lots of training data.





AI and Recidivism

If there’s one thing that’s infamous in US history, it’s racism. Conscious racism in the US seems to be on a gradual decline; but systemic racism is a residual with powerful consequences. In the case of an AI model performing a risk assessment, the results can often be unreliable and coded with bigotry. In the past, the legal system in the USA has not always been impartial; and, as a result, using past court cases and their results to shape an AI model’s algorithm is bound to reflect biases similar to those prevalent in the majority of the data the model is trained on. Race need not be named as a factor explicitly, because there exist many related traits (dialect, income, location, etc.) that show up in patterns of discrimination.


Regarding the story of Brisha Borden–a black, Broward County 18 year old with 4 juvenile misdemeanors–the abnormal results of her risk assessment invited further investigation that pointed to consistently unfavorable results for African-Americans. It was found not only that Blacks are likely to be rated more dangerous than they are, but also that Whites are likely to be rated less dangerous than they are. Alas, the roots of such problems exist far outside of recidivism (likelihood to reoffend) and far outside of 2014. This problem of AI prejudice is huge, and its impact is only growing as we further integrate AI into society.




Finding a Solution

It is widely accepted that the issue of bigotry in AI has no quick-fix. One reason for this is the idea that individuals hold many identities at once. This concept is called “intersectionality,” and it makes it harder to pinpoint the sources of bias and inequalities by compounding the effects of different biases related to the different identities that a person has.


A lack of a quick and easy solution doesn’t mean there’s no solution at all. In an interview, Boston University Research Scientist Dr. Nina Cesare explains, “addressing the training data is much lower hanging fruit, but in an ideal world we would fix the root cause as well and just get good training data.” Fixing the real-world problems that produce biased training data is possible, but likely inefficient; since it would take many years to create a model society and many more to produce enough data to train an AI model. In the meantime, it is more timely to correct the training data. Cesare claims, to do this job effectively, the group handling the problem needs to be more diverse than a group of just analytic experts: “we need the social scientists and the historians to … be critical of who created this data set and what kind of biases they … baked into it right?”. By including professionals from different fields in the conversation, we can better counteract prejudice in training data by understanding its source. Many times over, using workers from a variety of fields has enabled teams to locate causes of harmful biases in training data and minimize false positives.




Why does this matter?

Although ways to combat prejudice in AI models exist, these methods cannot remain shelved, because the effects of this issue will only grow worse with time. Left unchecked, AI models may not only perpetuate their biases, but also “learn” from themselves and “consider” their results correct in a way that exacerbates inequities.


AI models do not operate in a vacuum. Like children, they replicate what we “feed” them, and we “feed” them all the time. In healthcare and legal systems especially, underlying prejudice could be devastating. As a result of AI usage increasing in so many areas of society, it is urgent that we begin combatting harmful biases in AI models sooner rather than later; since they have been and will be making and influencing important, life-changing decisions.




Conclusion

It is important to understand that, while it may seem daunting, it is possible to eradicate or nearly eradicate “ingrained” prejudice in AI models. However, accomplishing this goal means starting as soon as possible, because society is on the clock. AI models are becoming more integrated into research and everyday life, so–unless we suddenly stop making mistakes–the issue will not disappear on its own. Tens of years of nonrepresentative training data can be replaced, but it takes real effort by many people. In the end, in order to prevent the ruin of innocent lives by unreliable, AI-powered decisions, it is important that we fight prejudice in AI in the ways we can. After all, anyone can help- so how will you?
